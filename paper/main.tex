\begin{abstract}
This working paper introduces a simulation framework for comparing supreme-court and constitutional-review designs. The model is not intended to forecast a real court. It is a design instrument for testing how institutional choices such as appointment method, court size, term length, recusal rules, emergency docket procedure, voting thresholds, panel structure, constitutional councils, dual-court cross-checks, and legislative overrides shape legal stability, rights protection, partisan alignment, legitimacy, and democratic responsiveness. The current implementation also models doctrine areas, policy domains, state/federal jurisdiction, lower-court hierarchy, dynamic justice replacement, public and legislative reaction dynamics, enforcement channels, documented external calibration profiles, uncertainty bands, scenario-specific institutional costs, and a split between emergency relief and merits invalidation. The project is separate from the legislative simulator, but can import legislative campaign outputs as subject-matter-preserving stress signals for generated constitutional-review dockets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation}

Constitutional review systems face a recurring design problem: courts need enough independence to enforce constitutional limits, but enough accountability and procedural discipline to avoid becoming unreviewable political actors. The classic countermajoritarian framing emphasizes the difficulty of judicial invalidation in a democracy \cite{bickel1962}, while comparative constitutional scholarship shows that constitutional review can be organized through many institutional forms rather than a single supreme-court template \cite{stoneSweet2000,ginsburg2003}. This project treats that design space as a simulation problem.

The goal is not to reproduce the United States Supreme Court, a European constitutional court, or any particular historical system. The goal is to compare mechanisms. A useful simulator should make it easy to ask whether a rule reduces shadow-docket abuse at the cost of administrative load, whether a supermajority invalidation rule improves stability while weakening rights protection, or whether a constitutional council improves legitimacy but becomes too deferential.

\section{Modeling Approach}

The simulator uses a modular Java architecture similar in shape to the legislative simulator, but keeps the court project independent. Each scenario is a named institutional design. A randomized world produces a docket of constitutional cases, and each scenario applies its own review process to the same docket.

Each case has a generated policy position, doctrine area, policy domain, jurisdiction, lower-court path, rights-threat signal, public support, legislative mandate, urgency, legal ambiguity, constitutional salience, lower-court conflict, lower-court panel skew, state-federal tension, intercourt conflict, certiorari pressure, time-to-review, executive pressure, conflict-of-interest risk, and public-trust context. These are deliberately stylized variables. They are intended to expose institutional tradeoffs, not to act as measured legal facts.

Each court scenario creates justices from the scenario's appointment method, court size, term and removal structure, independence level, accountability pressure, transparency level, coalition norm, rights priority, and stability preference. The review process then models review selection, emergency handling, recusals, panel or en banc review, voting thresholds, cross-checking court agreement, constitutional-council screening, legislative override, and opinion fragmentation. Runs are partitioned into review periods so term limits and removal or retention rules can change court composition over time rather than only at initial world generation. Decision outcomes also update an institutional reaction state: public trust, legislature-court conflict, court-curbing pressure, override pressure, amendment pressure, and compliance norms feed later cases in the same run. Enforcement is split into executive implementation, agency nonacquiescence, legislative reenactment, and local-government compliance. Each outcome also carries institutional budget, delay, and implementation-complexity costs so structural designs can be compared against resource constraints rather than only legal outputs.

\section{Metrics}

The first metric set is designed to keep the main tradeoffs visible. Higher values are better for legal stability, rights protection, legitimacy, democratic responsiveness, independence/accountability balance, compliance rate, executive implementation, and local-government compliance. Lower values are better for partisan alignment, shadow-docket abuse, emergency relief rate, merits invalidation rate, reversal rate, constitutional conflict, direct defiance, workaround behavior, agency nonacquiescence, legislative reenactment, legislature-court conflict, court-curbing pressure, concurrence fragmentation, dissent intensity, administrative load, institutional budget cost, institutional delay cost, implementation complexity, and total institutional cost. Review rate, merits review rate, repeated litigation rate, public trust, amendment pressure, recusal rate, panel/en banc rate, cross-check rate, council-screen rate, override rate, state-case share, mixed-jurisdiction share, lower-court depth, lower-court conflict, state-federal tension, intercourt conflict, time-to-review, and replacement pressure are diagnostic rather than directly good or bad.

The directional score is a display aid. It combines the directional metrics after inverting lower-is-better values. It should not be treated as a final welfare function. The individual metrics matter more than the aggregate score when diagnosing a design.

\section{Legislative Output Bridge}

The court simulator can use synthetic dockets or import legislative campaign CSV rows. Imported legislative outputs are converted into case-generation signals. Weak public mandate, low generated welfare, minority harm, concentrated-harm passage, lobby capture, public-preference distortion, policy shift, proposer gain, reversal activity, and status-quo volatility increase the likelihood of rights-threat, legal-ambiguity, salience, urgency, or conflict signals in the constitutional-review docket. If an imported row includes a policy-domain column, the generated review case preserves it; otherwise the importer infers a domain from row labels and stress signals. This keeps subject matter visible in policy-domain reports instead of collapsing legislative outputs into generic stress levels.

This bridge keeps the two projects separate. The legislative simulator remains responsible for generating institutional outputs from lawmaking rules. The court simulator treats those outputs as stress signals for review design.

\section{Starter Campaign}

The first campaign compares the core scenario set across five stress cases: baseline, polarized appointments, rights-threat surge, emergency-docket stress, and low-trust conflict. It writes aggregate, period-by-period, doctrine-specific, pipeline, policy-domain, composition, calibration, and uncertainty-band CSV files, a Markdown summary, and a provenance manifest under \texttt{reports/}. The starter synthetic campaign is useful for checking model behavior and report shape, but it is not empirical validation.

The second campaign is paired with the legislative simulator. It compares the same court designs against a synthetic baseline and four imported legislative docket modes: all imported rows, high-capture rows, high-volatility rows, and low-mandate rows. This makes the bridge operational: court designs can now be tested against outputs from different legislative systems without merging the two codebases.

The third campaign is a sensitivity check. It reruns the scenario catalog under high and low assumptions for emergency pressure, appointment polarization, rights-threat rate, public trust, and legislature-court conflict. These sweeps are not calibration in the empirical sense. They are a check on whether reported rankings are robust to plausible changes in the synthetic environment. Calibration profiles now live in \texttt{config/calibration/*.csv}, with \texttt{config/calibration-targets.csv} retained as a compatibility mirror. The current profiles use Supreme Court Database issue-area shares for 1946--2024 and 2000--2024 merits dockets \cite{scdb2025,scdbIssueCodebook}, SCOTUSblog's 2024 interim-docket page for emergency-docket context \cite{scotusblogInterim2024}, and Gallup court-confidence reporting for public-trust targets \cite{gallupCourtApproval2024}. Generated calibration reports compare observed values against those source-bearing profiles and include conservative 95\% bands.

Across the initial run, the current-style federal court has the strongest rights-protection score in several stress cases, but also shows higher shadow-docket abuse than designs with reason-giving or merits follow-up. The hybrid independence/accountability scenario has the highest directional score in the starter campaign because it combines reasoned emergency procedure, recusal substitution, supermajority invalidation, panel/en banc review, and a limited supermajority override. Cross-checking courts and constitutional councils improve stability and legitimacy in the synthetic run, but carry higher administrative load and lower reversal rates.

\section{Implemented Research Increments}

The current implementation now includes four research increments that move the simulator beyond the original starter campaign.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Documented calibration target files identify court, time period, target range, source name, and source URL instead of relying on undocumented starter ranges.
  \item Campaign, pipeline, composition, and calibration reports write conservative 95\% uncertainty bands. The bands are generated from aggregate denominators already present in the reports; a future raw-outcome export could support nonparametric bootstrap intervals.
  \item Imported legislative rows preserve policy subject matter through explicit or inferred policy domains, and campaigns write separate policy-domain diagnostic reports.
  \item Scenario outputs include institutional budget cost, delay cost, implementation complexity, and total institutional cost so designs such as expanded courts, dual courts, cross-checking courts, and constitutional councils can be evaluated against resource and delay constraints.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

The simulator starts from the premise that constitutional review is institutional infrastructure. The same constitutional values can be implemented through different combinations of appointment, tenure, docket control, voting thresholds, review structure, recusal discipline, emergency procedure, and override rules. A comparative simulator cannot settle constitutional theory, but it can make design tradeoffs explicit enough to argue about them with more precision.
